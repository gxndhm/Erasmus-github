## Abstract

The United Kingdom has positioned itself as a global leader in digital transformation, yet for significant portions of its population — particularly older adults from minority ethnic communities in cities like Birmingham — the digital transition is not an opportunity. It is an accelerating form of exclusion.

This article argues that the UK's current approach to digital inclusion is **structurally inadequate**: not because ambition is absent, but because the framework treats digital exclusion as a problem of access and skills alone, ignoring the cultural, linguistic, and institutional trust dimensions that determine whether marginalised communities engage with digital services in the first place.

**Keywords:** digital inclusion, digital exclusion, HCI, culturally adaptive design, Birmingham, minority ethnic communities, UK policy, ISO 25010, inclusive requirements elicitation

---

## Key Findings

- 🔢 **2.4 million** UK adults have never used the internet (Lloyds Bank Consumer Digital Index, 2023)
- 🔢 **5.4 million** lack basic digital skills for everyday online services
- 🏙️ **Birmingham** — with 40%+ minority ethnic population — illustrates national policy failures acutely
- ❌ The dominant **deficit model** of digital literacy treats excluded communities as the problem, not the system
- ✅ Nordic models (Estonia, Finland) demonstrate that **co-design and trust-building** produce genuinely inclusive outcomes
